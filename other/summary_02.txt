Why stolen credentials continue to work even where MFA is in place
============================================================

**Modern cyberattacks are increasingly bypassing traditional security by using "infostealer" malware to hijack active user sessions.**

**Key points**
*   **Beyond passwords:** Infostealer malware collects more than just login details; it steals browser history, autofill data, and "session cookies."
*   **Bypassing MFA:** By stealing session cookies, hackers can trick websites into believing they are the legitimate user, allowing them to bypass multi-factor authentication (MFA) entirely.
*   **The "Personal" risk:** Employees often use work emails on personal sites or log into work accounts from unsecure personal devices, creating easy entry points for hackers.
*   **Hard to detect:** Because attackers use valid credentials and recognized devices, their activity often looks like normal, legitimate behavior to security systems.
*   **Need for change:** Organizations must move toward "dynamic" security that monitors behavior throughout a session, rather than just checking identity at the initial login.

**Why it matters**
Traditional security measures like passwords and MFA are no longer enough because hackers are now stealing the "digital identity" of employees rather than just guessing their codes. This allows criminals to operate undetected within corporate networks, making it essential for companies to adopt more advanced, behavior-based security monitoring.
