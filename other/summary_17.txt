The IBM scientist who rewrote the rules of information just won a Turing Award
============================================================

**Quantum computing pioneers Charles Bennett and Gilles Brassard have been awarded the 2025 A.M. Turing Award for developing unbreakable, physics-based encryption.**

**Key points**

*   **A chance encounter:** The foundation for modern quantum cryptography began with a spontaneous conversation between Bennett and Brassard in a swimming pool in 1979.
*   **Information is physical:** The pair moved beyond viewing information as abstract data, proving that it must obey the laws of physics, specifically quantum mechanics.
*   **The "no-copy" rule:** Unlike digital files, quantum information cannot be copied without being altered; this means any attempt to eavesdrop on a quantum-encrypted message leaves an immediate, detectable trace.
*   **Unbreakable security:** Their "BB84" protocol allows two parties to share secret keys that are theoretically impossible to crack, even by future computers with unlimited processing power.
*   **Industry recognition:** This is the first time the prestigious Turing Award—often called the "Nobel Prize of computer science"—has been granted for research in the quantum field.

**Why it matters**
As powerful quantum computers threaten to break the mathematical codes currently protecting the world’s banking and government data, the work of Bennett and Brassard provides a vital, future-proof alternative for global digital security.
