I didn't know USB port colors meant something — now I plug devices into the right one every time
============================================================

Understanding the color-coding system for USB-A ports helps users identify data transfer speeds and power capabilities, though manufacturer inconsistencies often require consulting device manuals for accurate technical specifications.

### Key Points
- White ports indicate the original USB 1.0 standard, while black ports represent the faster USB 2.0 generation introduced in 2000.
- Blue ports signify USB 3.0, offering data speeds up to 5Gbps, whereas teal or red ports typically denote the 10Gbps USB 3.1 Gen 2 standard.
- Yellow or orange USB-A ports feature "Always-on" charging, allowing devices to charge even when the host computer is powered down.
- Manufacturers sometimes ignore standard color conventions for aesthetic reasons, such as matching ports to a motherboard's specific color theme.
- Users should reserve high-speed USB 3.0 and newer ports for external drives, while using older ports for low-bandwidth peripherals like mice or keyboards.

### Why it Matters
Properly identifying USB-A port capabilities ensures that high-performance hardware, such as external SSDs, receives the necessary bandwidth for optimal operation. Relying on visual cues alone can be misleading, so verifying port specifications through official documentation prevents performance bottlenecks and hardware compatibility issues.
