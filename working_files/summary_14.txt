What Is The One-Watt Rule For Standby Electricity?
============================================================

The International Energy Agency’s one-watt initiative establishes global standards to limit standby power consumption in household electronics, helping consumers reduce energy waste and lower their annual utility costs.

### Key Points
- The one-watt initiative, proposed in 1998 and standardized in 2010, mandates that appliances consume no more than one watt of power while in standby mode.
- Standby power usage accounts for 5% to 10% of total residential energy consumption, costing the average U.S. household more than $100 annually.
- Regulations apply to a wide range of devices, including TVs, game consoles, computers, and power adapters, to ensure manufacturers optimize energy efficiency.
- Always-on smart devices and voice assistants often challenge these efficiency limits because they require constant network connectivity to function.
- Consumers can identify energy-efficient products by looking for Energy Star certification, which indicates compliance with low-power consumption guidelines.

### Why it Matters
Reducing standby power consumption helps households lower their carbon footprint and mitigate rising electricity costs caused by peak usage and external market factors. By prioritizing energy-efficient devices, consumers can effectively manage their long-term utility expenses and support broader resource optimization goals.
