The FCC’s Spam Call Proposal Is Just a Data Collection Scheme
============================================================

The Federal Communications Commission is considering a proposal to require telecommunications providers to collect personal identification and address data from all customers to combat spam and scam calls.

### Key Points
- The FCC proposal mandates that users provide government-issued identification and a physical address to obtain or maintain a phone number.
- Critics, including the Electronic Frontier Foundation and the ACLU, argue the plan will fail to reduce spam, which largely originates from overseas.
- The policy would eliminate anonymous "burner" phones, potentially blocking access for domestic violence survivors, the unhoused, and those without stable documentation.
- Major telecommunications companies, including AT&T and Comcast, have suffered significant data breaches, raising concerns about the security of centralized personal information.
- Existing technical standards like STIR/SHAKEN are cited as more effective, privacy-preserving alternatives for addressing fraudulent robocalls.

### Why it Matters
This proposal represents a significant shift in digital privacy by effectively ending the ability to maintain anonymous communication lines in the United States. If enacted, it could create massive, vulnerable databases of sensitive consumer information while disproportionately disenfranchising marginalized populations who lack standard government identification.
