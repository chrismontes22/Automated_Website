Thunderbolt 5 finally makes eGPUs great — but it has one big, boring problem
============================================================

The new Thunderbolt 5 standard significantly increases bandwidth for external GPU enclosures, offering a high-performance alternative to Thunderbolt 4 while maintaining essential hot-plugging capabilities for portable laptop users.

### Key Points
- Thunderbolt 5 provides 80 Gbps of bidirectional bandwidth, doubling the 40 Gbps capacity found in the previous Thunderbolt 4 standard.
- The new interface supports a 120 Gbps asymmetric "boost" mode, though current eGPU controllers remain hardware-limited to 64 Gbps.
- Thunderbolt 5 offers a distinct advantage over Oculink by supporting hot-plugging, allowing users to connect and disconnect devices without restarting their systems.
- USB4 v2 serves as a compatible alternative to Thunderbolt 5, as both standards share similar underlying transmission technologies and performance specifications.
- High entry costs and limited hardware availability currently make Thunderbolt 5 enclosures a niche investment compared to more affordable Thunderbolt 4 or USB4 solutions.

### Why it Matters
The transition to Thunderbolt 5 represents a major step toward making external GPUs a viable desktop replacement for high-performance gaming and professional workloads. However, the current high cost of entry and limited device compatibility suggest that most users should wait for the technology to reach broader market adoption.
