Canada vows to amend Bill C-22's encryption and metadata rules amid massive tech backlash
============================================================

The Canadian government is amending the controversial Bill C-22 to clarify encryption and metadata rules following significant backlash from major technology companies and privacy-focused service providers regarding digital security.

### Key Points
- Public Safety Minister Gary Anandasangaree confirmed the government is drafting amendments to better define encryption and metadata requirements within the proposed legislation.
- Tech giants including Apple, Google, and Meta warned that the original bill could weaken global cybersecurity and facilitate foreign interference.
- Several VPN providers, such as Proton VPN, ExpressVPN, and NordVPN, threatened to exit the Canadian market if forced to compromise their no-logs policies.
- The original bill would have required electronic service providers to retain metadata for up to one year and comply with secret data-retrieval orders.
- Despite the revisions, the government maintains that updated lawful access tools are necessary for intelligence agencies to combat modern digital threats.

### Why it Matters
The outcome of this legislation will set a critical precedent for the balance between national security surveillance and the protection of user privacy in the digital age. If passed, the bill could force major global tech firms to choose between complying with Canadian law or withdrawing their services from the country entirely.
