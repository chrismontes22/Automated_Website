Data centers are booming. Indigenous leaders want help protecting their lands.
============================================================

Indigenous leaders are calling for greater oversight and consent regarding the construction of energy-intensive AI data centers that threaten local water supplies, sacred lands, and environmental stability globally.

### Key Points
- Hyperscale AI data centers can consume over 100 megawatts of power annually, equivalent to the electricity usage of 100,000 households.
- Research indicates U.S. data centers consumed approximately 17.4 billion gallons of water in 2023, with energy generation requiring an additional 211 billion gallons.
- Indigenous groups in countries including Mexico, Chile, Brazil, and the U.S. are challenging projects through protests, lawsuits, and municipal moratoriums.
- Some Indigenous nations, such as the Woodland Cree First Nation in Canada, are pursuing economic opportunities by taking majority stakes in data center projects.
- Organizations like Te Kāhui Raraunga in New Zealand are developing governance frameworks to ensure AI infrastructure aligns with Indigenous rights and digital sovereignty.

### Why it Matters
The rapid expansion of AI infrastructure creates a significant tension between global technological advancement and the protection of Indigenous land rights and natural resources. Establishing clear standards for consent and environmental impact is essential to prevent the marginalization of vulnerable communities as the demand for digital power continues to surge.
