Wrongful Arrest Exposes Failures in One of the Oldest Police Face-Recognition Tools in the US
============================================================

Florida resident Robert Dillon has filed a lawsuit against law enforcement agencies after a flawed facial recognition match led to his wrongful arrest for a crime he did not commit.

### Key Points
- Robert Dillon, a Florida crabber, was arrested in 2024 after the FACES system incorrectly identified him as a suspect in a child-luring incident.
- The FACES database, operated by the Pinellas County Sheriff's Office, provided a 93 percent match based on low-quality surveillance footage.
- Investigators allegedly ignored evidence that Dillon lived 300 miles away and that his vehicles were never present in the city where the crime occurred.
- The American Civil Liberties Union filed the lawsuit, which seeks damages and a mandatory overhaul of facial recognition policies for the involved agencies.
- This incident marks at least the 15th known wrongful arrest in the United States attributed to the use of facial recognition technology.

### Why it Matters
This case highlights the significant risks of relying on algorithmic matches as primary evidence in criminal investigations without sufficient human oversight or corroborating facts. It raises urgent questions about the accountability of law enforcement agencies and the potential for automated systems to cause irreparable harm to innocent citizens.
