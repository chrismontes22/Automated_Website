California Steps Back From Dangerous Expansion of its Age-Gating Law
============================================================

The California legislature has amended A.B. 1856 to remove provisions that would have expanded age-gating requirements to web browsers and websites, following concerns regarding user privacy and digital security.

### Key Points
- Assemblymember Buffy Wicks authored A.B. 1856, which now excludes browsers and websites from its expanded age-gating framework.
- The bill includes an exemption for open-source operating systems, leading the Electronic Frontier Foundation to withdraw its formal opposition.
- Existing law A.B. 1043, signed in 2025 and effective January 2027, mandates that operating systems and app stores categorize users by age.
- Companies face fines of up to $7,500 per affected child for failing to comply with the age-gating requirements established by A.B. 1043.
- Critics argue that these mandates incentivize invasive data collection, such as biometric scanning and mandatory ID checks, to verify user ages.

### Why it Matters
These legislative changes prevent the immediate expansion of age-gating mandates that could have further compromised online anonymity and data security. While the core requirements of A.B. 1043 remain, the decision to limit the scope of A.B. 1856 reflects a growing debate over the balance between protecting minors and maintaining a private, accessible internet.
