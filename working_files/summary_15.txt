Web Browsers on Video Game Consoles
============================================================

Video game consoles have integrated web browsers since the 1990s, evolving from rudimentary gateways for the technologically inexperienced into sophisticated, feature-rich applications before eventually fading from modern systems.

### Key Points
- Early attempts like the Philips CD-i and Sega Saturn’s Net Link provided basic internet access to bridge the digital divide for casual users.
- Browsers on consoles like the Dreamcast, PlayStation 3, and Wii U introduced advanced features including tabbed browsing, Flash support, and specialized controller-based navigation.
- Nintendo and Sony have largely moved away from general-purpose browsers on the Switch and PlayStation 5, restricting web access to system-level tasks like eShop logins.
- Microsoft remains an outlier, offering a full Chromium-based version of the Microsoft Edge browser on the Xbox One and Xbox Series consoles.
- Console browsers frequently served as vectors for homebrew development and custom firmware, often becoming targets for security exploits throughout their lifecycles.

### Why it Matters
The decline of general-purpose console browsers reflects a shift in consumer technology where smartphones and streaming dongles have rendered TV-based web browsing redundant. While these tools were once vital for connecting households to the early internet, they are now primarily relegated to niche utility or system-level authentication.
