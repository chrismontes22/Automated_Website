Think for Yourself
============================================================

A Wellesley College English professor explores the existential threat artificial intelligence poses to human creativity, arguing that the slow, deliberate process of writing is essential to genuine knowledge.

### Key Points
- The author, a department chair at Wellesley College, observes that AI tools like Google’s "Help Me Write" threaten the human capacity for original, associative thought.
- The essay contrasts the "active gaps" of human hesitation—seen in the manuscripts of Emily Dickinson—with the efficiency-driven, capitalist model of time promoted by big tech.
- The author highlights the decline of humanities departments and the loss of serious arts coverage as evidence of a broader cultural shift toward prioritizing immediate, marketable outcomes.
- The text references *The Book of Knowledge*, an early 20th-century encyclopedia, as a model for discovery-based learning that resists the search-optimized nature of modern databases.
- The author updated his classroom AI policy to acknowledge that detecting machine-generated text is increasingly difficult, shifting his stance toward creating intentional communities of human writers.

### Why it Matters
The rapid integration of AI into academic and professional writing risks eroding the human ability to engage in deep, time-intensive intellectual work. By prioritizing efficiency over the "unfinished" nature of human thought, society may lose the capacity for the original, critical inquiry that defines the humanities.
