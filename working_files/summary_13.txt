The NTSB tries to keep cockpit audio recordings private. AI is making that harder
============================================================

The National Transportation Safety Board temporarily removed thousands of investigative documents after artificial intelligence tools enabled the public to reconstruct restricted cockpit audio recordings from published sound-spectrum images.

### Key Points
- The NTSB pulled public dockets following the unauthorized reconstruction of cockpit audio from UPS flight 2976, which crashed in Louisville, Kentucky, last year.
- Investigators had published spectrograms—visual representations of audio frequencies—to help identify a mysterious ringing sound heard before the crash.
- Using existing AI tools, members of the public successfully converted these visual spectrograms back into audible sound files in approximately ten minutes.
- Federal law prohibits the NTSB from releasing actual cockpit voice recordings to protect pilot privacy and the integrity of safety investigations.
- While most dockets have been restored, 41 remain under review as the agency evaluates new safeguards to prevent similar digital reconstructions.

### Why it Matters
This incident highlights a significant vulnerability in government transparency as emerging AI technologies can easily bypass long-standing legal protections for sensitive investigative data. The NTSB must now balance its mandate for public disclosure with the need to prevent the unauthorized release of private, potentially traumatic audio from aviation accidents.
