Tech workers are spending nights and weekends learning new AI tools. They say they can't afford not to.
============================================================

Tech workers are increasingly dedicating significant personal time and money to mastering artificial intelligence tools outside of work hours to remain competitive in a rapidly evolving job market.

### Key Points
- An Ernst & Young survey found that 85% of US desk workers are learning to use AI tools during their personal time.
- Software engineers and product designers report spending 10 to 20 hours weekly on after-hours AI experimentation to avoid becoming technically obsolete.
- Professionals are paying out-of-pocket for subscriptions to tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and Cursor, with some spending thousands annually on training and software.
- LinkedIn data shows a surge in hiring for AI-specific engineering roles, while demand for traditional software engineering positions has remained flat or declined.
- Some employees, such as those at Amazon, report that internal company resources help, but heavy workloads still force most learning into evenings and weekends.

### Why it Matters
This trend highlights a growing "learning tax" where the burden of professional development shifts from employers to individual workers to keep pace with technological disruption. As AI continues to reshape industry standards, the ability to adapt quickly is becoming a critical factor in long-term job security and career advancement.
