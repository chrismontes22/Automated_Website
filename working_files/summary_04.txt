Tech companies desperately want to film you doing chores
============================================================

AI training startup Shift is offering free home cleaning services in New York and London in exchange for video footage used to teach robots how to perform domestic chores.

### Key Points
- Shift collects "egocentric" or first-person video data of cleaners performing tasks like scrubbing, dusting, and mopping to train physical AI models.
- Robotics companies face a data bottleneck because physical tasks involving friction, force, and spatial awareness are difficult to scrape from the internet.
- The startup Human Archive is scaling data collection by having gig workers record their activities while wearing specialized camera-equipped caps.
- Home services platform Pronto has faced backlash for recording footage of clients performing chores like cooking and laundry to develop AI training sets.
- Some companies operate "data farms" where workers are paid to repeat simple physical tasks, such as folding towels, while sensors capture their movements.

### Why it Matters
This trend highlights a shift toward commodifying private domestic behavior to overcome the significant technical challenges of training robots for the physical world. As companies race to automate household labor, the practice raises critical questions regarding consumer privacy and the ethics of using personal living spaces as data collection sites.
