Web Browsers on Video Game Consoles
============================================================

The evolution of web browsers on video game consoles reflects a decades-long transition from providing essential internet access to serving as specialized, restricted tools within modern gaming ecosystems.

### Key Points
- Early consoles like the Philips CD-i and Sega Saturn introduced rudimentary web access to position gaming hardware as affordable, TV-based alternatives to personal computers.
- The Sega Dreamcast and PlayStation 2 offered advanced browsing features, including support for Flash, email, and peripheral integration like keyboards and mice.
- Nintendo’s DS and Wii utilized Opera-based browsers, with the Wii U’s GamePad offering a unique dual-screen interface that allowed for independent web navigation.
- Microsoft’s Xbox consoles transitioned from Internet Explorer to a Chromium-based version of Edge, mirroring the desktop experience on the Xbox One and Series X/S.
- Modern systems like the PlayStation 5 and Nintendo Switch have largely abandoned general-purpose browsers, restricting web access to internal functions like eShops and authentication.

### Why it Matters
The decline of user-facing console browsers highlights how the ubiquity of smartphones and streaming dongles has rendered the "console as a home computer" concept obsolete. While these browsers were once vital gateways for the digitally underserved, they have shifted from being a primary utility to a niche feature often exploited for system modifications.
