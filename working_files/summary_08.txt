The 'papers, please' era of the internet will decimate your privacy
============================================================

New government-mandated age verification laws for social media are forcing users to surrender sensitive personal data, threatening online anonymity and privacy for citizens across the globe and America.

### Key Points
- Australia’s under-16 social media ban, effective December 2025, requires platforms to collect government IDs or biometric data to verify user ages.
- Research indicates the Australian ban has failed to significantly reduce adolescent social media use, with roughly 70% of minors still accessing platforms.
- Third-party verification services, such as Singapore-based k-ID, create significant security risks, evidenced by a recent Discord-related breach that exposed government ID images.
- The United Kingdom is planning an "Australia-plus" enforcement model, with officials considering potential restrictions on virtual private networks (VPNs) to prevent verification evasion.
- In the United States, over 19 states have passed age-verification legislation, while federal proposals like the Kids Online Safety Act (KOSA) threaten to mandate identity checks nationwide.

### Why it Matters
These policies shift the internet toward a "papers, please" model where users must be profiled and identified to participate in public discourse. This infrastructure of surveillance creates permanent risks for data breaches and government overreach, ultimately chilling free speech by eroding the ability to communicate anonymously.
