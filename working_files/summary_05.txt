If You Buy a New Router, It Might ‘Turn Into a Pumpkin’ Next Year
============================================================

The Federal Communications Commission has issued an unprecedented ban on new foreign-manufactured routers, effectively freezing the U.S. market and raising concerns regarding future security firmware and software updates.

**Key points**
- The FCC order restricts new routers manufactured, assembled, or designed outside the United States due to national security risks.
- Existing routers currently authorized for sale remain available, but manufacturers face uncertainty regarding future approvals and supply chain compliance.
- Essential security firmware and software updates for affected devices are currently slated to end on March 1, 2027.
- Cybersecurity experts warn that the broad scope of the ban may inadvertently leave consumers with vulnerable, unsupported hardware in the coming years.
- Most major router brands rely on international manufacturing, making it difficult for consumers to identify which future models will remain compliant.

**Why it matters: **
This regulatory shift creates significant uncertainty for consumers and businesses, as the potential loss of security patches could render millions of home and office networks vulnerable to cyberattacks. Until the FCC clarifies which manufacturers will receive exemptions or conditional approvals, experts advise delaying new router purchases to avoid investing in hardware that may soon become obsolete.
