SpaceX, Explained: 8 Things That Matter the Most After the IPO
============================================================

SpaceX achieved the highest-valued IPO in financial history on June 12, raising over $75 billion and reaching a $2 trillion valuation despite the company currently operating at a loss.

### Key Points
- SpaceX shares debuted at $135 and surged 19% on the first day of trading, though the stock has since experienced significant price volatility.
- Elon Musk retains 85% of the company's voting power through super-voting shares, despite owning approximately 42% of the total equity.
- The company reported $18.5 billion in revenue for 2025 but incurred a $5 billion loss, largely due to heavy investment in xAI data centers and aerospace equipment.
- A Nasdaq rule change allows SpaceX to enter the Nasdaq-100 index on July 7, potentially forcing the stock into millions of retirement and savings accounts.
- The IPO featured an unusually large 30% allocation for retail investors, though demand still outpaced supply during the initial offering.

### Why it Matters
The inclusion of SpaceX in index funds and potential government-backed children's accounts shifts the financial risk of a volatile, profitless company onto everyday families and retirees. This move sets a controversial precedent for how high-profile tech firms can bypass traditional vetting processes to gain immediate, widespread market exposure.
