AI Sovereignty and the Architecture of Participation
============================================================

Nations are increasingly pursuing technological and medical sovereignty by building domestic infrastructure to reduce reliance on centralized global supply chains and dominant foreign technology firms.

### Key Points
- Brazil is expanding its domestic vaccine and pharmaceutical manufacturing capacity through public institutions like Fiocruz and Butantan to ensure medical independence.
- Countries including India, Japan, and members of the EU are launching national AI initiatives to develop local compute infrastructure and reduce dependence on American and Chinese hyperscalers.
- Experts argue that true sovereignty requires physical infrastructure—such as data centers, power grids, and chips—rather than just access to open-source model weights.
- The current global trade model is criticized for concentrating value at central hubs, prompting a shift toward federated systems where participants retain more control.
- Industrial policy and public procurement are identified as essential tools for building the foundational capacity needed to support sovereign technology ecosystems.

### Why it Matters
This shift toward sovereign infrastructure represents a fundamental move away from the neoliberal model of globalized trade toward a more federated, decentralized economic architecture. By prioritizing local capacity, nations aim to prevent the "enshittification" and platform dominance that have historically allowed a few powerful entities to control essential technologies and resources.
