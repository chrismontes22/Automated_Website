Cybersecurity Mission Creep in the US
============================================================

A new academic paper identifies "cybersecurity mission creep," a trend where policymakers reframe diverse social and legal issues as urgent security threats to bypass standard legislative and public debate.

### Key Points
- Policymakers are increasingly labeling issues like misinformation, antitrust, and child safety as cybersecurity concerns to invoke a sense of urgency.
- The process of "cybersecuritization" allows officials to override competing policy considerations by framing problems as existential technological threats.
- This trend encourages excessive deference to technical specialists, which reduces transparency in governance and complicates public oversight.
- The paper argues that reframing complex social issues as security matters leads to oversimplified solutions and potential constitutional challenges.
- Researchers warn that failing to address this phenomenon risks shifting critical democratic decision-making away from the public and toward opaque security frameworks.

### Why it Matters
This trend threatens to erode democratic legitimacy by removing complex social debates from the public sphere and placing them under the control of technical experts. By treating policy challenges as security emergencies, governments risk implementing reductive solutions that bypass necessary constitutional and ethical scrutiny.
