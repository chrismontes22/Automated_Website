Solid-state batteries still aren’t ready, but gels are
============================================================

Semi-solid-state batteries are emerging as a safer, more durable alternative to traditional lithium-ion technology, gaining rapid adoption in e-bikes, power banks, and consumer electronics across global markets.

### Key Points
- Semi-solid-state batteries use a gel-like electrolyte that significantly reduces the risk of thermal runaway and combustion compared to volatile liquid electrolytes.
- Ride1Up’s Revv1 EVO e-bike, launching in August 2026, features a 1,040Wh semi-solid-state battery capable of 1,200 charging cycles.
- Global manufacturer Giant is integrating semi-solid-state technology into five upcoming e-bike models to increase energy capacity and reduce frame weight.
- Vivo has incorporated semi-solid-state electrolytes and silicon-carbon anodes into its X200 and X300 Ultra smartphone series.
- Stricter Chinese regulatory standards, including mandatory puncture testing for e-bike batteries, are accelerating the transition away from traditional liquid-based cells.

### Why it Matters
The shift toward semi-solid-state technology offers a practical, immediate solution to the fire hazards associated with lithium-ion batteries without requiring a complete overhaul of existing manufacturing infrastructure. As major manufacturers adopt these safer cells to meet rigorous international safety standards, consumers can expect longer-lasting, more reliable power sources across a wide range of portable devices.
