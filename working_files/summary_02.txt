The US Military’s GPS Software Is an $8 Billion Mess
============================================================

The US Space Force is considering canceling the troubled GPS Next-Generation Operational Control System program after RTX Corporation’s $7.6 billion project failed to become operational following delivery.

### Key Points
- The GPS Next-Generation Operational Control System (OCX) was intended to manage advanced GPS III satellites but remains nonoperational nine months after delivery.
- Originally budgeted at $3.7 billion with a 2016 completion date, the program's costs have ballooned to $7.6 billion, with an additional $400 million for future upgrades.
- Testing revealed extensive, unresolved technical issues across all subsystems, leading the Air Force to consider abandoning the project in favor of updating legacy systems.
- The program has faced 15 years of delays, software defects, and poor systems engineering, according to the Government Accountability Office and military officials.
- OCX was designed to fully enable jam-resistant "M-code" signals, which are critical for protecting military navigation against spoofing and jamming in conflict zones.

### Why it Matters
The potential cancellation of the OCX program highlights significant vulnerabilities in the military's ability to modernize critical navigation infrastructure against modern electronic warfare threats. Relying on aging legacy systems to manage advanced satellite capabilities limits the effectiveness of GPS-guided weaponry and increases the risk of signal interference in global conflict zones.
