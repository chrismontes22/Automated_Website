‘The moment infrastructure moves to orbit, that assumption disappears’: Layered access control, physical redundancy and rapid component replacement are major orbital challenges
============================================================

Major technology companies are exploring orbital data centers to bypass terrestrial power and land constraints, though experts warn that maintenance and security challenges remain significant hurdles for implementation.

### Key Points
- Hyperscalers are considering orbital infrastructure to meet the massive energy demands of AI, which currently exceed terrestrial grid capacity and available land.
- Unlike terrestrial data centers, orbital hardware lacks the ability for physical human intervention, turning routine repairs into potential months-long outages.
- Experts like Acre Security CEO Kumar Sokka highlight that current fault-tolerance models for satellites do not support the tightly coupled, interdependent workloads of AI compute.
- Launch costs make traditional N+1 redundancy strategies economically prohibitive, requiring a shift toward self-diagnosing systems and predictive maintenance.
- Orbital infrastructure faces unique environmental threats, including space debris, radiation, and extreme thermal conditions, which cannot be mitigated by standard physical security perimeters.

### Why it Matters
Moving AI infrastructure to space represents a fundamental shift in how companies manage critical compute resources and operational risk. If these systems become essential rather than supplementary, the inability to perform physical maintenance could create unprecedented vulnerabilities in global digital infrastructure.
