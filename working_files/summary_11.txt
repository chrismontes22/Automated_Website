Waymo called the cops on teen riders, raising privacy concerns
============================================================

Waymo alerted San Mateo police to two teenagers allegedly drinking and firing toy guns inside a driverless robotaxi, sparking a national debate regarding passenger privacy and corporate surveillance.

### Key Points
- San Mateo police detained two 15-year-olds after Waymo’s internal sensors detected suspicious behavior and remotely disabled the vehicle.
- Waymo vehicles utilize up to 29 cameras and various microphones to monitor both the interior cabin and the surrounding environment.
- Alphabet, Waymo’s parent company, reported receiving nearly 290,000 government requests for user data across its platforms during the first half of 2025.
- Privacy experts argue that passengers often lack clear information regarding the extent of data collection and how recorded audio or video may be utilized.
- A recent Pew Research Center poll indicates that 71% of Americans feel uncomfortable riding in autonomous vehicles, citing safety and privacy concerns.

### Why it Matters
This incident highlights the growing tension between public safety and individual privacy as autonomous vehicles become integrated into daily transportation. It forces a broader conversation about whether these cars function as private transit or as mobile surveillance platforms that could be leveraged by law enforcement.
