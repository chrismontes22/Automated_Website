‘Free’ World Cup streams are spreading scams, not soccer
============================================================

Cybercriminals are exploiting interest in World Cup soccer by hosting fraudulent streaming sites that infect user devices with malware, malicious advertisements, and unwanted subscription traps instead of live content.

### Key Points
- Security firm Malwarebytes identified a surge in fake World Cup streaming sites designed to deliver malware and phishing links.
- Malicious video players often feature hidden overlays and fake controls that trigger unwanted software updates or prize-winning scams.
- Experts recommend using official streaming partners, such as YouTube TV or the BBC, which maintain transparent business models and established reputations.
- Users should avoid unvetted third-party sites and verify the legitimacy of a service by checking its company history and terms of service.

### Why it Matters
These scams highlight the growing risk of "malvertising" where cybercriminals leverage high-traffic sporting events to compromise personal devices. By choosing verified, official streaming platforms, users can protect their data from unauthorized access and avoid falling victim to deceptive subscription schemes.
