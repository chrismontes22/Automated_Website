The Race to Build AI Data Centers — Before the People Can Protest
============================================================

Kevin O’Leary is facing intense public backlash over the proposed 40,000-acre Stratos data center project in Utah, which critics argue threatens local water supplies and exacerbates environmental degradation.

### Key Points
- The Stratos project covers 40,000 acres near the drought-stricken Great Salt Lake, an area more than double the size of Manhattan.
- Policy director Jim Walsh of Food and Water Watch warns the facility will increase reliance on the Ruby Pipeline, further incentivizing water-intensive fracking.
- Data centers currently consume roughly 176 terawatt-hours of electricity annually, with projections suggesting a massive increase to 580 terawatt-hours by 2028.
- The Trump administration is actively seeking to accelerate data center development by easing federal regulations and permitting requirements.
- Local communities in states like Georgia and Utah are increasingly enacting moratoriums to address concerns over water quality, pressure, and resource depletion.

### Why it Matters
The rapid expansion of AI infrastructure is creating a direct conflict between the tech industry’s energy demands and the essential resource needs of local communities. This tension highlights a growing national debate over whether the economic and strategic benefits of data centers justify the long-term environmental costs to public water and power grids.
