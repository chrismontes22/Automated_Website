Strategic Stability, Structural Strain | Sinification: May 2026
============================================================

Chinese analysts are debating the long-term implications of the May 2026 Xi-Trump summit, focusing on strategic stability, AI governance, and the structural challenges facing China’s domestic economy.

### Key Points
- Analysts are divided on stimulus strategies, with proponents favoring infrastructure investment while critics argue for consumption-based reforms like increased rural pensions.
- The US-China intergovernmental AI dialogue is viewed as a critical, narrow window for China to secure a role in global governance and industrial ecosystems.
- Scholars are debating a "dual-core multipolar" global order, where China and the US act as primary powers while allowing middle powers space to hedge.
- Concerns regarding China’s research culture have intensified following high-profile academic fraud cases, prompting calls for reforms to better cultivate original talent.
- Experts warn that Japan’s remilitarization and its evolving role in the US alliance system are becoming significant obstacles to regional stability and Taiwan policy.

### Why it Matters
These debates reflect a growing internal consensus that China must navigate a more fragmented global order while addressing deep-seated domestic economic and institutional imbalances. The shift in discourse highlights a strategic pivot toward "competitive coexistence" as Beijing attempts to balance its technological ambitions with the risks of international isolation.
