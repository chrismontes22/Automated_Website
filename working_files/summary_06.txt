Web Browsers on PDAS
============================================================

Early personal digital assistants (PDAs) pioneered mobile internet access through diverse operating systems and specialized browsers before the rise of modern smartphones rendered the dedicated PDA category largely obsolete.

### Key Points
- Early PDA connectivity relied on dial-up, infrared, and expansion cards before Wi-Fi and Bluetooth became standard in high-end devices by 2003.
- Major operating systems included EPOC, Apple Newton, Palm OS, Windows Mobile, and the Linux-based Sharp Zaurus.
- Browsers evolved from basic proxy-based tools like Palmscape and NetHopper to more advanced, native engines like Opera Mobile, NetFront, and Internet Explorer Mobile.
- Specialized software like Minimo and Fennec attempted to bring full desktop-class browsing experiences from Mozilla Firefox to resource-constrained PDA hardware.
- The transition from proprietary systems to Linux on devices like the Zaurus allowed users to run full desktop environments, effectively turning PDAs into portable mini-laptops.

### Why it Matters
The evolution of PDA browsers represents the foundational era of the mobile web, establishing the technical standards and user interface patterns that define today's smartphone experience. Understanding this history highlights the rapid shift from limited, proxy-dependent browsing to the powerful, standards-compliant mobile internet ecosystem we rely on today.
