Cybercriminals are cashing in on the World Cup by selling stolen streaming accounts
============================================================

The 2026 FIFA World Cup, hosted by the U.S., Canada, and Mexico, has reached record viewership levels while simultaneously fueling a massive surge in black-market streaming account sales.

### Key Points
- The 2026 tournament is the largest in history, featuring 48 national teams across 16 host cities in North America.
- Viewership records were broken during the semifinals, with Argentina’s win over England drawing 15.06 million viewers on Fox.
- HUMAN Security identified 12 million compromised streaming accounts on the dark web, representing $220 million in potential black-market value.
- On June 27 alone, threat actors released 802,000 stolen accounts, generating an estimated $14.8 million in single-day revenue.
- Streaming platforms like Fubo are increasing security measures, including monitoring for suspicious geolocation patterns and unusual account activity.

### Why it Matters
The rise in stolen account sales highlights how cybercriminals exploit high-demand live sporting events to profit from consumers seeking cheaper access to broadcasts. This trend forces streaming providers to invest heavily in advanced security and proactive monitoring to protect user data and intellectual property.
