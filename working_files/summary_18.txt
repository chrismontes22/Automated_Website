The PM has pledged to protect artists’ copyright. But without action on AI theft it is all hot air
============================================================

Prime Minister Anthony Albanese has pledged to protect Australian creators by ensuring they retain control over their work as AI companies continue to scrape content for model training.

### Key Points
- Prime Minister Anthony Albanese stated that using Australian books, music, art, or news to train AI without creator consent and compensation constitutes theft.
- Current Australian copyright law, governed by the 1968 Copyright Act, lacks specific provisions to address the unauthorized scraping of data for large language models.
- Potential government solutions include legislative amendments to mandate payments for AI training or launching lawsuits against multinational tech companies for copyright infringement.
- The Australian Society of Authors has expressed support for the government's stance, though creators remain concerned about the lack of immediate, concrete action.
- Unlike existing systems for educational use, no current framework requires AI companies to compensate Australian authors or artists for the use of their intellectual property.

### Why it Matters
This policy shift highlights the growing tension between the rapid expansion of AI infrastructure and the protection of intellectual property rights for local creators. Establishing a legal precedent for compensation could set a global standard for how nations regulate the use of copyrighted material in the development of generative AI.
