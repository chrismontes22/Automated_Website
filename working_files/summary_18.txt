Your laptop's USB port is probably slower than advertised — here's how to verify it
============================================================

Modern laptop users often face significant performance bottlenecks because identical-looking USB-C ports frequently support vastly different data transfer speeds, ranging from basic charging to high-speed Thunderbolt 5 capabilities.

### Key Points
- USB standards are notoriously confusing, with naming conventions like USB 3.2 Gen 2 and USB4 often masking the actual performance capabilities of a port.
- Theoretical speeds vary drastically, from 480Mbps on older USB 2.0 connections to 80Gbps or higher on advanced USB4 Version 2.0 and Thunderbolt 5 ports.
- Physical appearance is unreliable, as USB-C connectors can support different protocols, and cables themselves often lack the necessary wiring for high-speed data transfer.
- Users can verify port capabilities using Windows Device Manager, macOS System Report, or the `lsusb -t` command in Linux terminal environments.
- Certified cables, often identified by USB-IF logos or lightning bolt icons for Thunderbolt, are essential to ensure hardware does not throttle data transfer speeds.

### Why it Matters
Using the wrong port or an incompatible cable can reduce data transfer speeds by a factor of ten or more, severely impacting productivity for tasks like external storage backups. Understanding these hardware limitations ensures users can maximize their device performance and avoid unnecessary frustration with slow file transfers.
