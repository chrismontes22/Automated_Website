Primed for Malware: Stop Selling Compromised Android Devices
============================================================

Major online retailers like Amazon and Walmart face mounting pressure to address the systemic sale of uncertified Android devices pre-installed with malware that can compromise home network security.

### Key Points
- Researchers identified the BADBOX campaign, which affected 10 million uncertified devices running the Android Open Source Project.
- Compromised hardware includes various consumer electronics such as smart TVs, streaming devices, and digital picture frames.
- Malicious software is often hidden within custom firmware, making it difficult for average users to detect or remove threats.
- The FBI has issued warnings regarding streaming devices that promise free access to premium content, a common vector for malware distribution.
- Experts are calling for increased firmware transparency and greater accountability for original equipment manufacturers to prevent the spread of infected products.

### Why it Matters
These compromised devices pose a significant security risk by turning home networks into nodes for illegal activity and large-scale cyberattacks. Retailers must implement more robust anti-fraud measures to protect consumers from purchasing hardware that facilitates these persistent digital threats.
