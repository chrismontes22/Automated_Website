340 newspapers block Wayback Machine. What that means for future of internet...
============================================================

More than 340 U.S. news publishers have blocked the Internet Archive’s Wayback Machine from indexing their content, citing concerns that AI companies may use the archives for model training.

### Key Points
- Over 340 national and local news outlets, including The New York Times, have restricted access to the Wayback Machine.
- The majority of these blocked sites are local newspapers owned by five major conglomerates, primarily USA Today.
- Publishers argue that blocking the tool protects their proprietary content and ensures access remains limited to paying subscribers.
- Internet Archive director Mark Graham maintains that the library is not a source for commercial AI scraping and warns that blocking it harms the public record.
- A petition signed by over 200 journalists advocates for the tool's preservation, citing its necessity for historical research and digital investigations.

### Why it Matters
The restriction of the Wayback Machine threatens the long-term preservation of digital history, as nearly 40% of web pages from a decade ago are already inaccessible. This conflict highlights the growing tension between media companies protecting their intellectual property and the public's need for an open, verifiable historical record.
