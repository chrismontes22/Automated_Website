Canada’s Bill C-36 tackles AI privacy. Is it enough?
============================================================

Canada is introducing Bill C-36, a major legislative overhaul designed to modernize private-sector privacy laws and address the evolving risks posed by artificial intelligence and automated decision-making systems.

### Key Points
- Bill C-36 marks Canada’s first significant update to private-sector privacy legislation in over 25 years.
- The proposed law expands the definition of personal information to include data inferred by AI algorithms.
- New protections classify information belonging to individuals under 18 as inherently sensitive, granting minors stronger deletion rights.
- The legislation mandates greater transparency for organizations using automated systems to make significant decisions about individuals.
- The bill maintains existing exemptions for journalistic, artistic, and literary work to ensure public interest investigations remain protected.

### Why it Matters
This legislation attempts to shift the focus of privacy law from simple data collection to the more complex risks of AI-driven profiling and behavioral prediction. By balancing individual agency with the need for public-interest research, the bill sets a critical precedent for how governments can regulate technology that increasingly influences daily life.
