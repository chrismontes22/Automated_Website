Get with the times — here's what a 'Luddite' means today
============================================================

As artificial intelligence adoption accelerates, the historical term "Luddite" is being reclaimed by modern critics who argue that technology should prioritize human welfare over corporate profit and exploitation.

### Key Points
- The original Luddites were 19th-century English textile workers who protested the use of automated machinery to lower wages and erode labor standards.
- Between 1811 and 1817, workers destroyed factory equipment and sent threatening letters signed by the mythical figure "Ned Ludd" to demand better working conditions.
- The British government eventually suppressed the movement by deploying the army and making machine-breaking a capital offense punishable by death.
- Modern activists, including college-aged "Luddite clubs," are repurposing the label to express concerns regarding algorithmic control, social media, and Big Tech's societal influence.
- New York state is currently considering legislation to pause the development of data centers, reflecting growing public anxiety over the rapid expansion of artificial intelligence.

### Why it Matters
The resurgence of the Luddite label highlights a growing tension between rapid technological innovation and the preservation of labor rights and mental well-being. Understanding this history provides context for current debates over whether emerging technologies serve the public interest or primarily benefit corporate stakeholders.
