Privacy as a Fundamental Right? The Government’s Terrible Privacy Track Record Suggests Virtue Signalling Over a Genuine Commitment
============================================================

The Canadian government is preparing to introduce new privacy reform legislation this week, aiming to establish privacy as a fundamental right despite ongoing criticism regarding its current legislative record.

### Key Points
- The upcoming bill seeks to codify privacy as a fundamental right, a move previously proposed in the stalled Bill C-27.
- Critics argue the government’s actions, including Bill C-22 and Bill C-34, contradict this rhetoric by mandating metadata retention and age verification requirements.
- Bill C-22 proposes lowering evidentiary standards for accessing subscriber information and requires providers to store user communication data.
- Bill C-34 introduces an age verification mandate for social media platforms like Facebook, Instagram, and Reddit, raising significant data security concerns.
- The government has faced accusations of sidelining Privacy Commissioner Philippe Dufresne by blocking his testimony on key legislative amendments.
- Bill C-25 continues to face scrutiny for providing federal political parties with weaker privacy obligations than those required of other organizations.

### Why it Matters
The proposed legislation faces skepticism because it coincides with multiple government initiatives that critics claim actively erode digital privacy and weaken regulatory oversight. Whether this bill provides substantive protections or serves as symbolic policy will determine the future of data rights and government accountability in Canada.
