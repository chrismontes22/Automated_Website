Apple says latest conflict minerals review found no basis to link suppliers to armed groups
============================================================

Apple has submitted its annual conflict minerals disclosure to the SEC, confirming that its supply chain for products like iPhone and Mac avoids financing armed groups in the Congo.

### Key Points
- Apple’s report covers the 2025 calendar year and includes all major hardware, such as iPhone, Mac, iPad, Apple Vision Pro, and various accessories.
- The company mandates that 100% of identified smelters and refiners for tin, tantalum, tungsten, and gold (3TG) undergo annual independent third-party audits.
- Apple states it found no reasonable basis to conclude that its supply chain smelters or refiners directly or indirectly financed armed groups in the Democratic Republic of the Congo.
- The company maintains a policy to terminate business relationships with any suppliers or smelters that refuse to meet its responsible sourcing and audit standards.
- Apple acknowledges that it cannot always verify the specific country of origin for minerals due to its reliance on supplier-provided data rather than direct mine-site procurement.

### Why it Matters
This disclosure is a critical component of Apple's regulatory compliance and corporate social responsibility efforts regarding human rights in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. By maintaining these transparency standards, the company attempts to mitigate legal risks and reputational damage associated with allegations of forced labor and unethical mineral sourcing in the global tech supply chain.
